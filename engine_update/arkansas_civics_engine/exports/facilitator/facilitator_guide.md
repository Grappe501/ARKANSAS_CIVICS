# Arkansas Civics Facilitator Guide


# Civic Awakening


## Pulling Up More Chairs


### Opening Scene
**Coaching note:** Invite the room to picture a real local meeting space before discussing ideas.

Do not start with theory. Start with memory. Make participants see the room, hear the fluorescent lights, and picture the empty chairs.


### The Empty Room
**Coaching note:** Help participants name the cost of absence without shaming people for disengaging.

Avoid portraying disengaged people as lazy. Emphasize exhaustion, confusion, alienation, and previous bad experiences.


### The Quiet Civic Crisis
**Coaching note:** Invite people to distinguish between apathy and learned powerlessness.

Let participants name their own reasons for disengagement. Do not rush to solution mode too quickly.


### Pull Up More Chairs
**Coaching note:** Close the chapter with invitation

The emotional move here is from diagnosis to possibility.


## How Power Actually Works


### Where Power Lives
**Coaching note:** Help participants move from abstraction to named institutions.

Participants often jump too quickly to the governor or legislature. Keep them grounded in local structures first.


### Local Government Power
**Coaching note:** Use real local examples participants recognize.

If participants do not know their institutions, that ignorance is not failure. It is a sign of how inaccessible civic systems have become.


### Direct Democracy
**Coaching note:** Emphasize that ballot access is both a constitutional tool and a political battleground.

Do not oversimplify petition politics. Make clear that the tool exists, but access to the tool can be contested.


### The Arkansas Civic Power Map
**Coaching note:** This is the anchor visual. Reuse it throughout the course and workshop.

Return to this map often. It is one of the main memory devices in the suite.


## The Arkansas Civic Story


### Farmers, Populists, and Economic Pain
**Coaching note:** Use economic hardship as an organizing lens

Connect history to present conditions. Ask people what has changed and what has not.


### The Elaine Massacre as a Warning
**Coaching note:** Treat this with moral seriousness. Do not flatten it into an abstract lesson.

Ground the discussion in dignity, grief, and structural analysis. This is not a motivational anecdote.


### Labor, Unions, and Right to Work
**Coaching note:** Frame labor history as both economic history and civic training.

Keep the tone educational and strategic. This is both history and a movement design exercise.


### The Modern Civic Awakening
**Coaching note:** This section will later expand with real campaign stories

Leave room for first-person narrative and real campaign documents when this chapter expands.


## The Tools Citizens Already Have


### Ballot Initiatives and Referendums
**Coaching note:** Clarify the difference between proposing policy and referring a law already passed.

Stress that direct democracy is both empowering and contested.


### Voting Systems Shape Power
**Coaching note:** Help participants understand tradeoffs without forcing one reform conclusion too early.

This can become its own advanced course later. For now, keep it practical and grounded.


### At-Large Elections and Jim Crow Power
**Coaching note:** Tie structure to power

Make clear that procedural design is never merely technical. It is part of the architecture of power.


### Tools Become Movements
**Coaching note:** End the course section with agency and strategic imagination.

This is the bridge into Course 2. Make the reader feel invited, not overwhelmed.
