# Arkansas Civics Workshop Guide


# Civic Awakening


## Pulling Up More Chairs


### Opening Room Reflection
Ask participants to describe a real local room where decisions happen: a school board room, city hall, quorum court, planning commission, church board, neighborhood association, or workplace leadership meeting.


### Empty Room Discussion
Small-group question: What costs show up when the public room stays quiet? Ask groups to identify costs to trust, policy quality, fairness, and community relationships.


### Mapping the Quiet Crisis
Create a wall chart with three columns: `Why people care`, `Why people withdrew`, and `What would bring them back`.


### Invitation Circle
Closing round: Each person names one chair they are willing to pull up—for themselves, for a neighbor, or for a community voice that is usually missing.


## How Power Actually Works


### Power Mapping Exercise
Use a flip chart to map an issue upward from personal experience to neighborhood, local government, state government, direct democracy, and the courts.


### Local Power Inventory
Participants build a three-column chart: city, county, school. Under each, list what decisions are made there.


### Ballot Power Brainstorm
Small-group exercise: brainstorm one issue that belongs in the legislature, one that belongs in local government, and one that might justify direct democracy.


### Full Power Map Walkthrough
Display the map and ask participants to place sticky notes where they currently engage and where they want to engage next.


## The Arkansas Civic Story


### Shared Pain Mapping
Groups identify one local pain point and translate it into movement language.


### Reading Backlash Across Time
Facilitated discussion: What can modern organizers learn from movements that faced backlash without romanticizing the harm they endured?


### Labor Storyline Mapping
Map a hypothetical Arkansas Workers Voice movement from shared pain to ballot initiative.


### Modern Movement Timeline Exercise
Create a wall timeline of recent petition campaigns, then identify where each one faced institutional pushback.


## The Tools Citizens Already Have


### Ballot Access Decision Exercise
Facilitator presents three hypothetical issues. Participants decide which route—legislative lobbying, initiative, or referendum—fits each one best.


### Election Design Exercise
Run a mock election with three candidates under plurality, runoff, and ranked choice rules. Compare the outcome.


### Representation and Exclusion Discussion
Present a hypothetical city with a 40 percent minority community and a five-seat at-large council. Ask participants to compare representation outcomes under at-large and district systems.


### Tool-to-Movement Planning
Groups choose a hypothetical issue and build a first-draft movement pathway from problem to institution to tactic.
